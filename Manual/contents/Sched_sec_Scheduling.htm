Overview

Once Part I: Beginning steps are completed, it is time to start scheduling the rest of the classes. Scheduling means finding the best placements for each class. The best placement is the semester/term and day/period patterns where the teacher and room are available, and the fewest number of student conflicts exist.

 

The automatic scheduling tool (Master builder - Automatic) cannot schedule a class into a placement where the teacher is teaching another class or where the room is occupied by another class (unless the classes are intentionally grouped). This means that there is the potential for a better placement that would reduce student conflicts, but the teacher and/or room are occupied. The analysis tools will help identify classes with many student conflicts. That's when the manual scheduling tool (Master builder - Manual) will help you determine if there is a better placement if a different teacher or room where assigned to the problem class. Hint: read the overview section of Master builder - Manual for a better understanding of the information used to determine the best placement for a class.

 

There are 3 steps to scheduling classes into their best placement.  Typically, a school will schedule a small group of classes, repeating the 3 steps until satisfied with the schedule for that small group of classes.  Then another small group of classes are scheduled, again repeating the 3 steps until satisfied. These 3 steps are repeated for small groups until all classes are done:

 

 

Before scheduling classes, give some consideration to what small groups of classes may be scheduled and in what order.  The classes that are scheduled early in process have a lower change of introducing student conflicts, while classes scheduled later in the process will have fewer placements as they need to work around the previously placed classes.  Here are some small class group considerations:

 

  • Classes that have limited room options with high demands (e.g. classes that must be scheduled into a single gym or  computer lab or  science lab).
  • Courses that have only 1 class (aka single section classes).
  • Senior level classes where students have compulsory courses in order to be accepted into post-secondary programs.

 

Here are some example groups:

 

  • Group 1 = all classes starting with PE (if all physical education classes start with PE)
  • Group 2 = all single section classes
  • Group 3 = all grade 12 classes
  • Group 4 = all grade 11 classes
  • Group 5 = all grade 10 classes
  • Group 6 = all grade 9 classes

 

The school is 'scheduled' when:

 

  • all classes have semester/term patterns and day/period patterns
  • all classes have a teacher
  • all classes have a room
  • the final load students has been run and the majority of students have timetables (resolving individual student conflicts may still be needed).

 

Suggested approach to scheduling the school

 

1. Schedule classes

Use Scheduling next year > Schedule classes > Master builder - Automatic to schedule a small group of classes.

 

This step may be repeated after step 3. If step 3 identifies 'problem' classes, it will provide a quick link to Master builder - Manual which provides all the information to help you decide where to schedule the class, including the number of conflicts that would be generated in each potential placement. Manual placement may highlight a better location not available in the automatic mode because the teacher and/or room were unavailable.

 

If any class has a change to its schedule, you must go to step 2 so that the students timetables will reflect the changes.

 

2. Assign student to classes

Run Scheduling next year > Assign students to classes > Load students. This process automatically creates individual student timetables based on the master timetable and the student's course selections. This step must be run after any changes to class schedules.

 

3. Analyze master timetable

Run Scheduling next year > Analyze master timetable > Analyze conflicts to identify 'problem' classes. These classes might have a better placement if analyzed further using Master builder -manual which is accessed using the Schedule option that appears when you select a class.

 

Another tool to analyze the master timetable is available using Scheduling next year > Schedule classes > Timetable of specific group. Here are some examples of how this may help:

 

  • By looking at the total number of students in each period, you can sense whether the timetable is balanced. For example, typically all students in grade 9 have full timetables (no spares), and these students typically only take grade 9 subjects. After scheduling the grade 9 classes you can see the number of students in each period. Looking at the nominal class size, you will see the theoretical number of students in the class assuming classes are perfectly loaded without conflicts (e.g. 2 classes with 42 students requesting the course would show 21 students per class).The numbers should be fairy even across the periods. If one period has too few students, and another period has too many students, consider analyzing the impact of moving classes around by using Master builder - Manual on a class in an over-loaded period to see if it can be moved to an under-loaded period).  
  • By looking at the actual class size, you can identify classes that are overloaded (if load students (step 2) was not limited to the maximum class size).  In this case, the overloaded classes are scheduled in a great spot, so consider analyzing the impact of moving the under-loaded class to a better location (using Master builder - Manual).

 

Remember that you may use Scheduling next year > Classes > Snapshots to create a snapshot of the master timetable at any point while experimenting with schedule changes. Snapshots may be restored allowing you to return to the master schedule to the way it was when the snapshot was created. If a snapshot is restored, you must run load students (step 2) again.

 

If any classes are rescheduled based on the analysis of the master timetable, you have done step 1 (schedule classes) again, so proceed to step 2 after all class changes are finished.

 

After scheduling this group of classes, create a Snapshot when you are satisfied with the master timetable, then proceed to step 4.

 

4. Repeat steps 1 to 3

Repeat steps 1, 2 and 3 for the next group of classes.  This step is repeated until all groups of classes are scheduled.

 

5. Check for unscheduled classes

Run Scheduling next year > Schedule Classes > Unscheduled classes to see if there are any classes that are not yet scheduled. Students cannot be assigned into these classes. If classes are found, you may select the class to access the schedule option which will open Master builder - Manual, for the class.

 

If any classes are scheduled here, return to step 2 so that students will get scheduled into these classes.

 

6. Assign rooms and teachers

Every class should have a teacher and a room.  Use Scheduling next year > Classes > Classes to access the list of classes. Sort by the teacher or room column to find classes with without teachers or rooms.

 

In order to avoid creating teacher or room conflicts, we suggest that these classes are assigned teachers and/or rooms using the features in Master builder - Manual where you can clearly see available teachers and rooms for the selected class. When the class is presented in Master builder - Manual, select the block that is identified as "current placement" (make sure you don't select any other placement so that you don't accidently reschedule the class necessitating the return to step 2). This will open the scheduling tools as shown below. Select the 'Available teachers' or 'Available rooms' field to see teachers and rooms that available for this class during this placement. If you select a teacher or room, icons appear to view the teacher's timetable and to see room information.  Check the Set as new teacher for class or Set as new room for class, then select the X in the Scheduling tools card (or the CLOSE button on the master builder form and then select the SAVE button when prompted.

 

.

 

 

Repeat this process until all real classes have teachers and rooms (placeholder classes like spares don't require this info).

 

Another option for assigning teachers and rooms is to use the options Scheduling next year > Classes > Available teachers or Available rooms to identify what's available for a specific semester/term/day/period.

 

Manual changes to teachers and rooms that are done without the use of the scheduling tools have the potential to introduce teacher or room conflicts. A conflict is when the teacher is scheduled to teach 2 or more non-grouped classes at the same time, or a room is occupied by 2 or more non-grouped classes at the same time.  Confirm that there are no teacher conflicts by running Scheduling next year > Analyze master timetable > Teacher conflicts. Confirm that there are no room conflicts by running Scheduling next year > Analyze master timetable > Room conflicts.

 

Use Scheduling next year > Classes > Teacher master timetable and Room master timetable to review all timetables quickly.

 

7. Add any other classes

Sometimes, schools like to add other classes into the student's timetables after the timetabling is completed. For example, if every student has a blank in period 3 for lunch, some schools add a placeholder lunch class into the timetable.  There are tools that may assist with the mass addition or removal of classes that may be available to assist with this functionality.

 

These tools may also assist K-12 schools where the elementary students do not have course selections and so their classes are added after the secondary students are scheduled.

 

Scheduling next year > Assign students to classes > Add classes

Scheduling next year > Assign students to classes > Delete classes

Scheduling next year > Assign students to classes > Clear timetables

 

Note: if step 2 is run again, all timetable changes made using the above tools will be lost.

 

8. Proceed to Part III:  Ending steps

The school is 'scheduled' when:

 

  • all classes have semester/term patterns and day/period patterns
  • all classes have a teacher
  • all classes have a room
  • the final load students has been run and the majority of students have timetables (resolving individual student conflicts may still be needed).

 

At this point, proceed to Part III: Ending steps.
